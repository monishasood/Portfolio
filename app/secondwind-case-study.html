The problem

NIL opened the door for athletes to get paid. The market behind it was chaos.

College athletes could finally earn from their name, image, and likeness, but there was no shared system to make it work. Deals lived in DMs, spreadsheets, and email threads. Athletes did not know what they were worth. Schools could not track compliance. Brands had no clean way to find and vet talent.

SecondWind wanted to be the platform that connected all three. When I joined, there was no product yet. Just the thesis and a small team.

Who had it

Three groups, three different pains.

Athletes

Did not know their market value or where to find legitimate deals.

Schools and compliance offices

Needed visibility and a paper trail, and were drowning in manual tracking.

Brands and partners

Wanted vetted athletes and a way to run campaigns without chasing people one by one.

Building for all three at once is the trap. The real first decision was whose problem we solved first.

Discovery

I talked to 30+ schools before writing a single feature.

The goal was not to validate an idea I already liked. It was to find the one workflow painful enough that people would change their behavior for it.

One pattern kept surfacing: partner-school onboarding and compliance was the bottleneck. Athletes would sign up, but schools could not keep up with verification and tracking, so adoption stalled at the institution level. If schools did not trust the data, nothing downstream mattered.

The call

I cut the marketplace from the MVP and built the school workflow first.

The decision

The team wanted to build the exciting side first: the athlete marketplace and brand discovery, the public-facing product that demos well. I argued for the opposite. We deprioritized the consumer marketplace and shipped the school side first: onboarding, verification, and a single source of truth for athlete data.

I ran the prioritization through RICE so the call was defensible, not a gut feeling. Reach and confidence both pointed at the school workflow. A two-sided marketplace does not work if one side never shows up, and schools were the harder side to win.

What I gave up

A flashier first demo, and the brand-facing features slipped a cycle.

What I got back

Schools actually adopted it, because we solved their problem instead of ours. That is where the 40% adoption came from.

What shipped

A working MVP that 500+ Division I athletes were onboarded onto.

  • A school onboarding and verification flow that compliance offices could actually run day to day.
  • A single source of truth for athlete data, replacing scattered spreadsheets and inboxes.
  • Airtable and Zapier automation that cut manual onboarding and data entry by 70% across 100+ institutions, and lifted data accuracy by 25%.
  • I led a cross-functional team of 8 through it, from roadmap to launch.

The hard side showed up, and the rest followed.

What I would do differently

Two things I would change next time.

  • Instrument the product sooner. The school-first call came from interviews, which was right, but I leaned on qualitative signal longer than I should have for the next set of decisions. Earlier analytics would have let me prioritize the second wave with data instead of debate.
  • Write the brand-side spec in parallel. I deprioritized it, but I should have kept it drafted and ready, so the moment school adoption landed, the next phase was ready to build instead of starting cold.

The throughline of how I work: do the unglamorous thing that makes the glamorous thing possible. Win the hard side of the market first.
